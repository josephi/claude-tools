# Structural patterns to avoid

Sentence and paragraph shapes that flag prose as AI-generated. Each has a fix.

## Binary contrast — "Not X. Y."

The AI-tell form is a denial followed by an assertion, usually as one-sentence paragraphs.

> Not an optimization. Not a tweak. A fundamental rewrite.

**Fix**: state Y directly.

> This is a fundamental rewrite.

## Negative listing

> Not a product. Not a feature. Not a framework. A philosophy.

**Fix**: name the thing.

> This is a philosophy.

## Dramatic fragmentation

Short fragments stacked for manufactured emphasis.

> Speed. Simplicity. Predictability. That's the tradeoff.

**Fix**: one sentence that says what it is.

> The tradeoff is speed and simplicity at the cost of flexibility.

## Self-posed rhetorical questions answered immediately

> The result? Devastating. The scope? Enormous.

**Fix**: fold the question into a statement.

> The result was devastating across the whole dataset.

## Tricolon abuse

Three-item lists stacked for rhetorical rhythm, not for accuracy.

> We built it to be fast, simple, and powerful.

If the three items aren't genuinely distinct, pick one or two.

> It's fast and simple.

## Anaphora

Three-sentence runs starting with the same word. Fine once, grating by the third.

> The system was slow. The system was fragile. The system was expensive.

**Fix**: vary the subject or combine.

> The system was slow, fragile, and expensive.

## Listicle disguised as prose

> The first wall is X. The second wall is Y. The third wall is Z.

**Fix**: if you genuinely have a list, use a bullet list. If you're using this structure for rhetoric, drop it.

## Passive voice with inanimate agents

> The complaint was transformed into a fix.
> The request becomes a response.

**Fix**: name the actor.

> The team turned the complaint into a fix.
> The server responds.

## Superficial participle analysis

Tacked-on `-ing` phrase that adds no information.

> …solving the problem, highlighting its importance.
> …reducing costs, illustrating the value.

**Fix**: cut the participle or replace with a specific claim.

> Solving it saved us four weeks.

## "Despite these challenges…"

Formulaic transition from negative to positive.

> Despite these challenges, the team pushed forward and delivered…

**Fix**: rewrite with the specific outcome or cut the whole transition.

> The team delivered in six weeks, three days under the deadline.

## Pedagogical voice

> Let's break this down.
> Think of it as…
> Imagine you're trying to…

**Fix**: skip the setup. Make the point.

## Fractal summary

Saying what you'll say, saying it, then summarizing what you said. Triple dilution.

**Fix**: just say it.

## Paragraph that ends with a one-liner punchline

> …and that's what happened.
>
> Game over.

One-liner paragraphs for drama work once per document, maybe. Use sparingly.

## Bold-first bullets

Every list item starting with a bolded keyword followed by a colon.

- **Speed**: The system is fast.
- **Reliability**: The system is reliable.
- **Scale**: The system handles scale.

**Fix**: if the bolded keyword adds information, keep it occasionally. If every bullet in a list has one, drop them.

## Em dashes

Strong AI signal. Replace with comma, period, or parenthesis.

> The feature — which took three months to build — is finally live.

→

> The feature (three months to build) is finally live.
> The feature is finally live. It took three months.

## Unicode arrows

`→` in prose is an AI tell (acceptable in lists or diagrams). Use "to" or "becomes".

## Three-or-more consecutive sentences of similar length

Break one.

## Paragraph that opens with "The" three times in a row

Same fix — vary openings.
